Risk free rate?

It is the return you'd get on a "zero risk" investment — typically a government bond (like US Treasury bills).
In options pricing, it matters because of the time value of money. If you're going to receive a payoff in the future, you need to discount it back to today's value.

Example: An option pays $100 in 1 year. With a 5% risk-free rate, that $100 in the future is worth $100 / 1.05 ≈ $95.24 today.

Black-Scholes uses it directly in the formula.
Monte Carlo uses it to simulate how the stock price drifts over time.
Without it, you can't price anything — you'd have no baseline for what money is worth across time.
