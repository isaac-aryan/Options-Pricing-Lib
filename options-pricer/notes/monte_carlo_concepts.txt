MONTE CARLO SIMULATION
======================

The core idea: instead of solving a problem analytically, you simulate it many, many times with random inputs
and average the results. As the number of trials grows, your average converges to the true expected value.


ROULETTE INTUITION
------------------
Imagine a roulette wheel:
  - Spin it 100 times → there's high variance around your winnings/losings. You might be up a lot or down a lot.
  - Spin it 1,000,000 times → the % return converges to the true expected value (near 0 for a fair wheel),
    and most runs of a million spins will land very close to that expected value.

The more trials you run, the tighter your results cluster around the truth. This is the whole reason Monte Carlo
works for option pricing: simulate thousands of possible stock price paths, compute the payoff on each,
average them, discount to today → that's your option price.


LAW OF LARGE NUMBERS (LLN)
--------------------------
Formal statement:
  In repeated independent trials with the same actual probability p of a particular outcome on each trial,
  the chance that the fraction of times that outcome occurs differs from p converges to zero as the number of
  trials goes to infinity.

Plain English: if you flip a fair coin enough times, the fraction of heads WILL get arbitrarily close to 0.5.
Not exactly 0.5 — but the probability of it being "far off" shrinks to 0 as trials → infinity.

Why this matters for Monte Carlo pricing:
  The LLN is the mathematical guarantee that our simulated average payoff converges to the true expected payoff.
  Without LLN, there'd be no reason to believe that running more simulations gives us a better answer.


GAMBLER'S FALLACY (what LLN does NOT say)
-----------------------------------------
The gambler's fallacy is the belief that if an outcome has happened more often than expected recently, it's
"due" to happen less often in the near future — i.e., that past outcomes influence future independent outcomes.

Example: you spin a fair roulette wheel and get 10 reds in a row (probability ≈ 1/1024).
  Gambler's fallacy says: "the next 10 spins will have more blacks to EVEN it out."
  This is WRONG. Each spin is independent. The wheel has no memory. The probability of red on spin 11 is
  still exactly the same as it was on spin 1.

LLN does NOT say the universe balances short-term streaks. It says that over a LARGE ENOUGH number of trials,
the FRACTION converges. The absolute difference between counts can actually grow — it's the RATIO that stabilizes.


REGRESSION TO THE MEAN (RTM)
----------------------------
Following an extreme random event, the next random event is, on average, less likely to be as extreme.

Classic example: if someone's parents are both taller than average, their child is likely to be taller than
average too — but closer to the mean than the parents were. Extreme outcomes tend to be followed by less
extreme ones, simply because extreme outcomes are rare.

Roulette example (the subtle one):
  You just got 10 reds in a row. What do you expect for the NEXT 10 spins?

  Gambler's fallacy: "I'll get fewer than 5 reds to balance out the streak." (WRONG)
  RTM (correct):     "I'll probably get fewer than 10 reds — the expected number is still 5."

  The key distinction: RTM doesn't say "you'll get LESS than the mean to compensate." It says "the next
  outcome will likely be closer to the mean than the previous extreme one was." The expected value of the
  next 10 spins is still 5 reds — it's just that 5 is a lot less extreme than 10.

  You might get 7 reds in the next 10 (still somewhat extreme), and after THAT the next 10 would again be
  expected to regress toward 5. But you don't bet on "fewer than 5 reds" just because the last streak was high.

Why this matters: RTM is a statement about EXPECTED values after extremes, not about some cosmic balancing
force. The gambler's fallacy confuses these two and leads to bad betting (and bad intuition about randomness).


CONFIDENCE INTERVALS (brief recap from MIT lecture)
---------------------------------------------------
When you estimate a quantity by averaging N random samples, your estimate has uncertainty.
A 95% confidence interval is a range such that, if you repeated the experiment many times,
roughly 95% of the computed intervals would contain the true value.

For a sample mean with sample standard deviation v and N samples:
  standard error (SE) = v / sqrt(N)
  95% CI = [mean - 1.96.SE,  mean + 1.96.SE]

The 1.96 comes from the standard normal distribution — it's the value such that ~95%
of the probability mass lies within ±1.96 standard deviations of the mean.

Key property: doubling your accuracy requires QUADRUPLING N. This is why naive Monte
Carlo is slow, and why variance reduction techniques exist.


GEOMETRIC BROWNIAN MOTION (GBM) - THE STOCK PRICE MODEL
--------------------------------------------------------
Before Monte Carlo can simulate anything, we need a mathematical model of how stock
prices evolve. That model is Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):

  dS = μ.S.dt + σ.S.dz

where:
  dS    = tiny change in stock price over tiny time dt
  μ     = drift rate (real-world expected return, e.g., 0.10 for 10%/year)
  σ     = volatility (std dev of returns, e.g., 0.25 for 25%/year)
  dz    = Wiener process increment — a tiny normally-distributed random kick

Two parts:
  μ.S.dt   = DETERMINISTIC drift. The stock drifts upward at rate μ.
  σ.S.dz   = RANDOM shock. The size of the noise is controlled by σ.

Why does μ multiply S (and not just stand alone as μ.dt)?
  Investors care about PERCENTAGE returns, not absolute dollar changes. A $1 move on
  a $10 stock is 10%, but on a $1000 stock it's 0.1%. What's constant across stocks
  is the expected PERCENTAGE return (dS/S)/dt = μ. Rearranging gives dS = μS.dt.
  Same logic applies to σ. This is why the process is called "GEOMETRIC" (multiplicative)
  rather than "arithmetic" (additive).

Consequence: stock prices under GBM can approach zero but never cross it. This matches
reality — stocks can't go negative.


THE MARKOV PROPERTY
-------------------
GBM is a Markov process: the future distribution of the stock price depends ONLY on
its current value, not on how it got there. Yesterday's price, last week's price, last
year's price — none matter once you know today's price.

This is the mathematical version of the "weak form of market efficiency": past prices
don't contain information useful for predicting future prices.

Important distinction (don't confuse these two):
  - The STOCK'S MOVEMENT is Markov (memoryless)
  - But an OPTION CONTRACT can still be PATH-DEPENDENT — i.e., its payoff at maturity
    can be defined to look at the whole history of the stock

  Example: an Asian option pays max(average(S) - K, 0). The stock itself is still
  Markov (each simulation step only depends on the previous one), but the CONTRACT
  reaches back and asks "what was the average over the whole period?" — so to compute
  the payoff, you need to record the full path.

  For a EUROPEAN option (what this project prices), the payoff only depends on S(T).
  So we don't care about intermediate values — we can jump straight from S(0) to S(T)
  in one step.


PARAMETERS: μ AND σ
--------------------
μ = expected annualized rate of return (μ=0.10 means "10% per year on average")
σ = annualized volatility (std dev of ANNUAL RETURNS, not of the stock price itself)

Both are in "per year" units. They scale DIFFERENTLY with time:
  - Drift scales LINEARLY with time:       over time t, expected drift = μ.t
  - Volatility scales with sqrt(time):     over time t, std dev = σ.sqrt(t)

This is a key property of Brownian motion. It's why long-term investing is "safer"
in relative terms: expected return grows linearly but uncertainty grows more slowly
(as sqrt).


THE LOGNORMAL SOLUTION (the key formula for simulation)
--------------------------------------------------------
Solving the GBM SDE analytically (using Itô's lemma) gives a closed-form formula for
the stock price at time T given its initial value:

  S(T) = S(0) . exp[(μ - σ^2/2).T  +  σ.sqrt(T).Z]       where Z ~ N(0,1)

Equivalently:
  ln(S(T)) ~ Normal( ln(S(0)) + (μ - σ^2/2).T,   σ^2.T )

This is why we say "stock prices are lognormally distributed" — it's a consequence
of GBM, not a separate assumption.

** THIS IS THE FORMULA YOU WILL CODE IN THE MONTE CARLO PRICER **

One random draw of Z → one sample of S(T). Repeat N times, average the payoffs,
discount by e^(-rT). That's the entire Monte Carlo pricer for a European option.

The -σ^2/2 term (the "convexity correction"):
  Why isn't the exponent just μ.T? Because when you exponentiate a normally-distributed
  variable, the expected value gets an extra +σ^2/2 bump. Property of the lognormal
  distribution: if X ~ N(m, s^2) then E[exp(X)] = exp(m + s^2/2). To keep E[S(T)] equal
  to S(0).exp(μT) — which is what "drift rate μ" should actually mean — we SUBTRACT
  σ^2/2 from the drift inside the exponent.

  This σ^2/2 term is the exact same one that shows up in d1 and d2 of Black-Scholes.
  Not a coincidence — it's the fingerprint of the lognormal-GBM math showing up in
  both pricers.


μ vs r: REAL WORLD VS RISK-NEUTRAL WORLD
-----------------------------------------
μ and r are DIFFERENT numbers describing DIFFERENT things:

  r = the risk-free rate — a MARKET FACT you can look up. Yield on a Treasury bill.
      If r=0.04 and you invest $100 in T-bills, you get exactly $104 in a year.
      Objective, observable, risk-free.

  μ = a stock's real-world expected return — a BELIEF or FORECAST. Different analysts
      give different numbers for the same stock. Subjective, estimated.

For risky stocks, μ > r because investors demand compensation (a "risk premium") for
holding something uncertain vs. holding a safe Treasury. The difference μ - r is the
equity risk premium (historically ~5-7% for the broad stock market).

Example numbers:
  r = 0.04  (Treasury yield — a fact)
  μ = 0.10  (expected Apple return — an estimate based on risk premium)

Nobody confuses these in normal finance. They describe clearly different things.


RISK-NEUTRAL VALUATION
----------------------
Here's the weird/beautiful result at the heart of all option pricing:

  To price an option, you can PRETEND that stocks drift at r (the risk-free rate)
  instead of μ (their real expected return). The price you compute under this fictional
  assumption is the CORRECT price in the real world.

This isn't an approximation — it's a rigorous result called risk-neutral valuation.
The fictional world where stocks drift at r is called the "risk-neutral world" or
"risk-neutral measure."

Why this works (hand-wavy intuition):
  Options can be REPLICATED by dynamically trading the underlying stock and a risk-free
  bond. By no-arbitrage, the option's price must equal the cost of replicating it.
  And the cost of replication doesn't depend on what anyone THINKS the stock will
  return — it only depends on observable quantities: r, σ, S, K, T. So μ drops out
  of the pricing formula entirely.

  (The rigorous proof uses Girsanov's theorem. You don't need it to USE the result,
  just to understand WHY it holds at the deepest level.)

Practical consequences are everywhere:
  - Black-Scholes takes r and σ as inputs, but NOT μ
  - Monte Carlo uses r as the drift in the GBM equation, not μ
  - Binomial trees use "risk-neutral probabilities," not real-world ones
  - All three models compute the same thing: the discounted expected payoff in the
    risk-neutral world

** In our code, we NEVER need μ. We only use r. **

When Hull writes the GBM equation as dS = μ.S.dt + σ.S.dz in Ch. 14, that's describing
reality. When he switches to dS = r.S.dt + σ.S.dz in Ch. 21, that's the risk-neutral
version used for pricing. Same equation, drift swapped.


THE MONTE CARLO PRICING RECIPE (Hull 21.6)
-------------------------------------------
For a European option on a non-dividend-paying stock:

  1. Sample a random path for S in a risk-neutral world
  2. Calculate the payoff from the derivative at maturity
  3. Repeat steps 1 and 2 many times (N simulations)
  4. Average the payoffs → estimate of E*[payoff] (risk-neutral expected payoff)
  5. Discount by e^(-r.T) to get the present value

For a European option, "sample a random path" collapses to "sample a random S(T)" —
because the payoff only depends on the terminal value, we use the one-shot formula
(Hull eq. 21.17):

  S(T) = S(0) . exp[(r - σ^2/2).T + σ.sqrt(T).Z]       where Z ~ N(0,1)

** Note: we substituted r for μ compared to the GBM formula from Ch. 14 **

In pseudocode:

  sum_payoffs = 0
  for i in 1..N:
      Z = sample from N(0,1)
      S_T = S_0 * exp((r - 0.5*sigma^2)*T + sigma*sqrt(T)*Z)
      sum_payoffs += payoff(type, S_T, K)     // max(S_T-K,0) or max(K-S_T,0)
  mean_payoff = sum_payoffs / N
  price = exp(-r*T) * mean_payoff

That's the entire pricer for a European call or put. About 10 lines of C++.


ONE-STEP VS MULTI-STEP SIMULATION
----------------------------------
Hull presents TWO versions of the discrete GBM formula:

  Multi-step (Hull eq. 21.16): simulate step-by-step with time increments Δt
    S(t+Δt) = S(t) . exp[(r - σ^2/2).Δt + σ.sqrt(Δt).Z]

  One-step (Hull eq. 21.17): jump directly from S(0) to S(T)
    S(T) = S(0) . exp[(r - σ^2/2).T + σ.sqrt(T).Z]

For EUROPEAN options, USE THE ONE-STEP VERSION. You only need S(T) for the payoff,
so simulating intermediate values is wasted computation producing the same final
answer.

The multi-step version is only required for PATH-DEPENDENT options (Asian, barrier,
lookback, etc.) where the contract reads the full trajectory. Those are NOT in the
scope of this project (Phase 2 is European calls and puts only).


STANDARD ERROR AND CONFIDENCE INTERVALS OF THE ESTIMATE
--------------------------------------------------------
Monte Carlo gives you an estimate, not the exact price. You need to quantify how
uncertain the estimate is.

  v  = sample standard deviation of the N (discounted) payoffs
  M  = number of simulations
  SE = v / sqrt(M)                       (standard error of the mean)
  95% CI = [mean - 1.96.SE,  mean + 1.96.SE]

To increase accuracy:
  - Halve the error → 4x more simulations
  - 10x more accurate → 100x more simulations
  - This O(1/sqrt(N)) convergence is slow — motivates variance reduction techniques
    (antithetic variates, control variates) covered in Hull 21.7

Cross-validation strategy for this project:
  Run Monte Carlo with the same inputs as Black-Scholes. Compute the 95% CI. If the
  Black-Scholes price falls INSIDE the CI, the Monte Carlo pricer is working correctly.
  If it doesn't, there's a bug.

Typical trial counts: 10,000 to 1,000,000 for reasonable accuracy on a European option.
Hull's Business Snapshot 21.2 uses 1000 trials and gets mean=4.98 vs BS=4.817, with
95% CI [4.51, 5.45] — BS falls inside, pricer works.


CONNECTION TO BLACK-SCHOLES (the big picture)
----------------------------------------------
Black-Scholes, Monte Carlo, and Binomial Tree are three different techniques for
solving the SAME underlying model: Geometric Brownian Motion in a risk-neutral world.

  Black-Scholes: solves it ANALYTICALLY     → closed-form formula
  Monte Carlo:   solves it by SIMULATION    → draw random S(T), average payoffs
  Binomial Tree: solves it by DISCRETIZATION → step through a probability tree

All three should give (approximately) the same price for a European option. This
is the foundation of cross-validation in this project.

Tradeoffs:
  - Black-Scholes: fastest and most accurate — but only works for vanilla European
    options with constant parameters
  - Monte Carlo: slow but extremely flexible — handles any payoff function, any
    stochastic process, path dependence, high-dimensional problems
  - Binomial Tree: middle ground — flexible enough to handle early exercise
    (American options), which MC struggles with
