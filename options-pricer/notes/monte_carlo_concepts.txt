MONTE CARLO SIMULATION
======================

The core idea: instead of solving a problem analytically, you simulate it many, many times with random inputs
and average the results. As the number of trials grows, your average converges to the true expected value.


ROULETTE INTUITION
------------------
Imagine a roulette wheel:
  - Spin it 100 times → there's high variance around your winnings/losings. You might be up a lot or down a lot.
  - Spin it 1,000,000 times → the % return converges to the true expected value (near 0 for a fair wheel),
    and most runs of a million spins will land very close to that expected value.

The more trials you run, the tighter your results cluster around the truth. This is the whole reason Monte Carlo
works for option pricing: simulate thousands of possible stock price paths, compute the payoff on each,
average them, discount to today → that's your option price.


LAW OF LARGE NUMBERS (LLN)
--------------------------
Formal statement:
  In repeated independent trials with the same actual probability p of a particular outcome on each trial,
  the chance that the fraction of times that outcome occurs differs from p converges to zero as the number of
  trials goes to infinity.

Plain English: if you flip a fair coin enough times, the fraction of heads WILL get arbitrarily close to 0.5.
Not exactly 0.5 — but the probability of it being "far off" shrinks to 0 as trials → infinity.

Why this matters for Monte Carlo pricing:
  The LLN is the mathematical guarantee that our simulated average payoff converges to the true expected payoff.
  Without LLN, there'd be no reason to believe that running more simulations gives us a better answer.


GAMBLER'S FALLACY (what LLN does NOT say)
-----------------------------------------
The gambler's fallacy is the belief that if an outcome has happened more often than expected recently, it's
"due" to happen less often in the near future — i.e., that past outcomes influence future independent outcomes.

Example: you spin a fair roulette wheel and get 10 reds in a row (probability ≈ 1/1024).
  Gambler's fallacy says: "the next 10 spins will have more blacks to EVEN it out."
  This is WRONG. Each spin is independent. The wheel has no memory. The probability of red on spin 11 is
  still exactly the same as it was on spin 1.

LLN does NOT say the universe balances short-term streaks. It says that over a LARGE ENOUGH number of trials,
the FRACTION converges. The absolute difference between counts can actually grow — it's the RATIO that stabilizes.


REGRESSION TO THE MEAN (RTM)
----------------------------
Following an extreme random event, the next random event is, on average, less likely to be as extreme.

Classic example: if someone's parents are both taller than average, their child is likely to be taller than
average too — but closer to the mean than the parents were. Extreme outcomes tend to be followed by less
extreme ones, simply because extreme outcomes are rare.

Roulette example (the subtle one):
  You just got 10 reds in a row. What do you expect for the NEXT 10 spins?

  Gambler's fallacy: "I'll get fewer than 5 reds to balance out the streak." (WRONG)
  RTM (correct):     "I'll probably get fewer than 10 reds — the expected number is still 5."

  The key distinction: RTM doesn't say "you'll get LESS than the mean to compensate." It says "the next
  outcome will likely be closer to the mean than the previous extreme one was." The expected value of the
  next 10 spins is still 5 reds — it's just that 5 is a lot less extreme than 10.

  You might get 7 reds in the next 10 (still somewhat extreme), and after THAT the next 10 would again be
  expected to regress toward 5. But you don't bet on "fewer than 5 reds" just because the last streak was high.

Why this matters: RTM is a statement about EXPECTED values after extremes, not about some cosmic balancing
force. The gambler's fallacy confuses these two and leads to bad betting (and bad intuition about randomness).
